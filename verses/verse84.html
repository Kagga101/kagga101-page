Kagga 101 – Verse #84

This verse explores the profound and multifaceted concept of Māyā, which is central to Hindu philosophy, particularly in the Advaita Vedanta tradition. It reflects on the interplay between the illusory world and the ultimate reality, touching upon themes of illusion, transcendence, and the nature of existence.

"Māyā, her grandeur limitless, lures me with her appearance,"
The verse begins by personifying Māyā as a grand, alluring force. Māyā refers to the illusion or appearance of the material world, which masks the true, unchanging reality of the universe. Māyā entices and captivates, creating the illusion of separateness and permanence in the ever-changing world. This aligns with the Bhagavad Gita's depiction of Māyā as the deluding power that veils the truth.

"But then what of that which transcends all attributes, her true essence?"
The poet now reflects on the essence behind Māyā—Brahman, the absolute, formless, and unchanging reality that underlies all appearances. In Advaita Vedanta, Brahman is beyond all names and forms, the source from which Māyā arises. While Māyā dazzles the senses, Brahman remains the silent, eternal truth that is beyond comprehension or perception.

"If she ceases, so do this world and the narrative of life."
This line affirms that Māyā is the fabric of the apparent world and the drama of existence. Without Māyā, the illusion of separateness would dissolve, and the world as we know it would vanish. This resonates with the idea that the world of multiplicity is a projection of Brahman through the lens of Māyā, and the end of Māyā reveals the oneness of reality.

"She's the mother, the projection of Brahman, and I her child!"
The verse concludes with a metaphor of relationship—Māyā as mother and the individual soul as her child. Though we are entangled in illusion, we are still rooted in Brahman. The child is not separate from the source; it is merely unaware. This encapsulates the Advaitic understanding that Atman (the individual self) is ultimately one with Brahman, and that the perception of separation is only due to the veil of Māyā.

Philosophical and Spiritual Context

  • Advaita Vedanta: The verse echoes Advaita Vedanta's core teaching that the world is an illusion (Māyā), and only Brahman is real. Through self-inquiry and realization, the seeker transcends Māyā to unite with the absolute.
  • Māyā: Māyā is the power of illusion that veils Brahman and creates the world of names and forms. She is not separate from Brahman, but a manifestation of its creative aspect.
  • Bhagavad Gita: Krishna teaches that those who are deluded by Māyā fail to see the truth, but those who gain knowledge through discipline and devotion can pierce the illusion and attain liberation (moksha).

Conclusion:

This verse poignantly illustrates the relationship between Māyā and Brahman. While Māyā lures us with illusion, she is also a divine expression of the One Reality. To realize that Māyā is a projection and not the ultimate truth is the beginning of awakening. The verse invites us to see through the illusion and recognize our identity not as separate beings, but as one with the eternal Brahman.

Kagga 101 – Verse #15

This verse engages deeply with philosophical and spiritual ideas related to emptiness, existence, and consciousness. It seems to challenge the notion of nothingness or emptiness in the context of self-awareness, and it touches on ideas that are common in Buddhist philosophy as well as other non-dual teachings.

"What’s it that says it’s all empty and nothing exists?"

This opening line poses a classic existential or philosophical question. It refers to the idea of emptiness—the notion that nothing in the world has inherent, independent existence. The verse questions this view, asking: If everything is empty, who or what is making this claim? The very statement of emptiness or nothingness suggests the presence of a conscious observer, which opens up a paradox.

"But then, won’t the one, conscious of emptiness, still exist?"

This line reveals the paradox. If nothing exists, how can there be an awareness that recognizes this? The awareness that perceives emptiness must still exist. This suggests a subtler form of existence—consciousness—that transcends both being and non-being.

"Trusting as the truth, take refuge in this pure awareness"

Here, the verse offers guidance. It points to pure awareness—untainted, unconditioned consciousness—as a foundation for truth. In spiritual traditions, awareness is often considered the ultimate reality that exists beyond conceptualization, judgment, or dualities. This line encourages trust in that awareness.

"Since it’s null and empty, this argument of nothingness!"

The final line challenges the argument of nothingness itself. Pure awareness is described as "null and empty" not in the sense of lacking value, but in the sense of being free from fixed concepts. Thus, the debate around nothingness becomes meaningless in the face of this direct, non-conceptual experience of awareness.

Overall Interpretation:

This verse is a philosophical meditation on the nature of emptiness and awareness. It explores the paradox that arises when we try to argue about emptiness: if nothing exists, who is aware of it? The verse suggests that awareness exists beyond these debates—it is pure, empty, and constant.

It encourages trust in awareness as a refuge—an unshakeable truth not bound by conceptual arguments. While all things may be transient or empty, awareness remains unchanging. True understanding and liberation come not from intellectualizing but from directly resting in this awareness that transcends all dualities.

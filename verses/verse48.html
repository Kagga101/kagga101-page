Kagga 101 – Verse #48

This verse explores impermanence and eternity in a philosophical and spiritual context. It contrasts the temporary nature of things with the timeless essence that underlies them. Let’s break down the themes:

"Deities are created and destroyed, but divinity lives on."

The first line acknowledges the transitory nature of deities and their manifestations, which rise and fall over time. The mention of creation and destruction emphasizes the impermanence of these forms. However, the verse immediately contrasts this with the idea that divinity, or the essential divine essence, is eternal and unaffected by these fluctuations. This line points to the distinction between the temporary physical forms (gods, idols, or representations) and the timeless reality or source of all existence, which remains constant.

"Days and nights come and go, but the present is permanent."

This line emphasizes the flow of time and the cyclical nature of existence. Days and nights—representing the passage of time—are constantly changing. But the present, the moment in which we experience life, is permanent in the sense that it is always now. The present is the only true reality; the past is already gone, and the future has yet to arrive. This idea echoes the philosophical concept of the eternal now, where time, though seemingly fluid, is understood as constantly centered in the present moment.

"The life of a person is ephemeral, but its essence is eternal."

This line highlights the contrast between the temporary nature of individual life and the eternal essence that resides within all beings. Ephemeral life refers to the physical existence—the body, the experiences, and the span of time we have. But the essence of a person—often understood as the soul, spirit, or consciousness—is eternal and unaffected by the fluctuations of time. This is a common theme in spiritual traditions, where the true self is considered to be beyond birth and death, existing in a state of timelessness.

"Yet, my focus is on the façade, not the bare absolute!"

The verse concludes with a personal realization or admission: the speaker recognizes that, despite understanding the impermanence of forms and the eternity of essence, their attention is still focused on the external—the surface-level façade—instead of the underlying truth or the bare absolute. This reflects a human tendency to become distracted by appearances, the world of forms, and the illusion of separateness. The speaker’s focus on the façade can be seen as a metaphor for how people often get caught up in the material world, seeking fulfillment in transient things, rather than recognizing the deep, unchanging reality that lies beneath all forms.

Interpretation:

The verse invites reflection on the nature of reality and perception. It contrasts the ephemeral with the eternal—the temporary forms of deities, days, and life, with the unchanging essence that transcends these forms. The focus on the façade at the end suggests a kind of spiritual ignorance or distraction, where the individual may understand the concepts of impermanence and eternity, but still remains attached to the surface-level experience rather than diving deeper into the absolute truth that transcends all forms.

Philosophical Implications:

This verse reflects deep philosophical and spiritual teachings that can be found in traditions like Advaita Vedanta and Buddhism. Advaita Vedanta teaches that the world is an illusion (Maya), and the only real truth is the non-dual self, which is eternal and beyond all forms. Similarly, Buddhism acknowledges the impermanence of life (Anicca) and encourages a deeper understanding of the true nature of self, beyond the transient world of appearances.

In both cases, the idea is that spiritual awakening involves seeing beyond the material forms and recognizing the oneness of existence—the eternal essence that underlies all things. The façade is the world of appearances, while the bare absolute is the true self, which is beyond form and time.

Conclusion:

The verse is a poignant reflection on the tension between the transient and the eternal. It suggests that while we live in a world of ephemeral experiences, the essence of life, and of ourselves, is eternal. However, our focus often remains on the surface, on the impermanent aspects of existence, rather than recognizing the deeper reality. This awareness calls for spiritual growth, a shift in focus from the external to the internal, from the façade to the essence, and ultimately toward the realization of the absolute truth that lies beyond the world of appearances.

Kagga 101 – Verse #48

"Deities are created and destroyed, but divinity lives on."

This line highlights the impermanence of specific forms of worship or religious representation, contrasting it with the eternal essence of divinity that persists beyond those forms.

"Days and nights come and go, but the present is permanent."

Time is always in motion, yet the present moment remains the only true constant. This line reflects the concept of the eternal now—a spiritual insight into presence and awareness.

"The life of a person is ephemeral, but its essence is eternal."

This contrast reminds us that while our bodies and lives are temporary, our spirit or essence—our deeper being—is timeless and infinite.

"Yet, my focus is on the façade, not the bare absolute!"

The speaker admits to being distracted by surface appearances rather than recognizing the eternal truth. It’s a humble acknowledgment of spiritual forgetfulness and misdirected attention.

Interpretation:

The verse invites us to shift our attention from transient forms and symbols to the eternal truths they point toward. It’s a reflection on how we often get caught up in what is seen, while neglecting what is essential.

Philosophical Insight:

This resonates with Vedantic thought and Buddhist impermanence—urging us to transcend Maya (illusion) and realize Brahman (absolute truth). It speaks to the heart of spiritual awakening.

Conclusion:

Verse 48 is a call to re-focus—from outer appearances to inner truths, from the temporary to the timeless. It encourages us to recognize the divine essence that persists beyond form, beyond time, and beyond illusion.

Kagga 101 – Verse #4

This verse explores the tension between the quiet, natural processes of growth and the human desire for recognition. The poet contrasts the subtle, effortless way nature works—grass growing, fruit ripening, and the sun and moon giving their light—with the human instinct to boast when achieving even the smallest accomplishments. The question of whether nature makes a spectacle of itself ("a drum roll," "a horn," "pomp and show") highlights the inherent simplicity of natural cycles, which happen without fanfare or celebration.

1. Dharma and the Natural Order

In Hinduism, dharma refers to the cosmic law, the natural order, and one’s duty or righteousness. The verse’s depiction of the natural world – grass growing, fruits ripening, the sun and moon shining – is an embodiment of dharma. These events happen as part of the natural order, without the need for recognition or external celebration.

In the context of Hindu philosophy, these natural processes reflect the unimpeded flow of dharma, which operates effortlessly and naturally. The grass grows because it is its dharma to do so, just as the sun shines because it fulfills its role in the cosmic order. The question the verse raises — "Is there a drum roll?" — points out how humans tend to celebrate small personal achievements or fleeting gains while nature itself operates with calm purpose, without the need for applause.

2. Karma and the Law of Cause and Effect

Karma in Hinduism refers to the law of cause and effect, where every action has consequences, whether immediate or in the future. The verse contrasts nature’s quiet unfolding with the human desire for recognition when small personal accomplishments are achieved.

The natural processes of growth, ripening, and celestial movements are themselves part of karma, the unfolding of actions and consequences in a harmonious, self-sustaining cycle. Nature's workings are unaffected by ego or desire, and this reflects the concept of karma in its purest form. It operates according to its own inherent laws and rhythms, regardless of human expectation. The verse’s mention of the speaker’s desire to "crow" about personal achievements highlights the tendency to seek recognition for one’s actions, which often implies an attachment to the fruits of one's labor — the opposite of selfless action, as taught in the Bhagavad Gita's concept of nishkama karma (action without attachment to results).

3. Ahamkara and the Illusion of the Self

In Hinduism, ahamkara refers to the ego or the sense of individual self that creates the illusion of separation from the rest of existence. The verse’s line "my throat wants to crow" illustrates how the human ego seeks to assert its importance and demand attention, even for small personal gains.

This yearning for recognition is a manifestation of ahamkara, which binds individuals to the transient and impermanent world of ego. According to Hindu philosophy, the ultimate goal is to transcend ahamkara and realize the true, unchanging nature of the self (Atman). The natural world, by contrast, operates without ego or desire for recognition — it simply is, reflecting the idea that nature’s cycles are expressions of Brahman, the ultimate reality, without the need for individual assertion.

4. Maya and the Illusion of External Validation

Maya refers to the illusion of the physical world and the false identification with the material, impermanent aspects of life. The verse draws attention to the fleeting nature of personal achievements that provoke the desire to boast. The "measly gains" the speaker refers to can be seen as a reflection of the illusion of Maya, where temporary accomplishments or external validation are given more weight than they truly deserve.

The desire to "crow" or announce these small victories may arise from the illusion of Maya, where individuals believe that external success or recognition is what gives their life meaning. In contrast, nature, which is free from Maya, proceeds in its own quiet rhythm, unaffected by ego or external validation. The verse thus contrasts the human attachment to fleeting successes with the natural world’s silent embodiment of the eternal truth.

5. Nishkama Karma (Selfless Action)

In the Bhagavad Gita, Lord Krishna advises Arjuna to perform his duties without attachment to the fruits of his actions — a concept known as nishkama karma. The verse’s line about "measly gains" highlights how humans, unlike nature, often perform actions motivated by desire for personal gain and recognition.

Nature, however, performs its functions without any attachment to success or failure, as seen in the steady growth of grass and the steady movement of the sun and moon. This selfless action, devoid of any desire for acknowledgment, embodies the ideal of nishkama karma. The verse contrasts the selfless actions of nature with the ego-driven actions of humans, encouraging the reader to reflect on the need to act with detachment, in alignment with the cosmic flow of dharma.

Conclusion:

This verse can be seen as a meditation on the contrast between the natural, selfless flow of life and the ego-driven desires of humans. Nature's processes — the growth of grass, the ripening of fruit, the shining of the sun and moon — all unfold according to dharma without the need for recognition or celebration. The speaker’s urge to boast about their own achievements, however, highlights the human tendency to be caught up in ahamkara, the illusion of self, and the desire for external validation, which ultimately distracts from the deeper truth of moksha and the recognition of one’s oneness with the universe. The verse encourages a return to a more humble, selfless existence, in harmony with the natural rhythms of the cosmos, free from the bonds of ego and attachment.
